## 1. Overview

This hackathon is organized by Claude Builder Club — part of the CBC Spring 2026 Global
Hackathon spanning 78 universities across 12 countries. You don't need prior experience
or domain knowledge — just bring curiosity and the willingness to build.

Over 24 hours online, you will form a team, choose a track focused on real social
impact, and build a practical AI-powered solution. We're looking for tools that solve
specific problems for specific people — whether in health, education, mental wellbeing,
governance, or creative expression.

You'll collaborate asynchronously, use Claude as your primary build partner, and have
something real to show at the end. The best builds will represent our campus on the
global stage.

## 2. The Five Tracks

Pick one track. Your project will be judged within that track. Choose the track that
best fits the problem you want to solve.

###### 2.1 TRACK 1 — BIOLOGY & PHYSICAL HEALTH

The problem:
Your doctor sees your vitals once a year. Your fitness app sees your workouts. Your
wearable sees your sleep. None of them talk to each other. No platform today has a
complete view of a person's health.

Examples:
Unified health intelligence system · Cross-signal correlations · Personalised risk modelling ·
Health history aggregator

Key ethical question:
What data is genuinely necessary versus noise — and what is the best way to make
longitudinal health data explainable?

###### 2.2 TRACK 2 — NEUROSCIENCE & MENTAL HEALTH

The problem:
There aren't enough therapists. Stigma prevents people from seeking help. Quality care
is gatekept by insurance, geography, and language. Many people suffer without
understanding what's happening to them.

Examples:
Evidence-based therapeutic support · Mental health literacy · Burnout prevention · Peer support
facilitation · Tools for recognising when professional help is needed

Key ethical question:
What can AI therapy do, and what can it absolutely not replace?


###### 2.3 TRACK 3 — ECONOMIC EMPOWERMENT & EDUCATION

The problem:
Talent is universal. Access to quality education and economic advancement is not.
Geography, wealth, and language lock people out of opportunity.

Examples:
Adaptive tutoring · Career transition guidance · Financial literacy · Job interview prep ·
Scholarship matching · Practical language learning

Key ethical question:
Who gets excluded by your design — by tech requirements, literacy level, or cultural
assumption?

###### 2.4 TRACK 4 — GOVERNANCE & COLLABORATION

The problem:
Democratic institutions are strained. Polarisation makes dialogue difficult. Civic
participation is down. Communities can't coordinate around shared challenges.

Examples:
Community deliberation platforms · Policy explanation tools · Legislative transparency ·
Grassroots organising · Conflict mediation

Key ethical question:
How could your tool be weaponised for manipulation? What's your safeguard?

###### 2.5 TRACK 5 — CREATIVE FLOURISHING

The problem:
As AI handles routine work, questions about human purpose become urgent. Access to
creative development is unequal. People who could find meaning through creative
expression often don't get the chance.

Examples:
Community storytelling platforms · Music/art education with real-time feedback · Writing
development tools · Language preservation · Intergenerational knowledge exchange

Key ethical question:
Is your tool keeping humans as creators, or quietly replacing them?

### 5. Judging Criteria

##### Every project is scored out of 100 points across four dimensions.

##### IMPACT POTENTIAL 25 PTS

```
21 – 25 Addresses a specific, meaningful problem with a clear population in mind. The benefit is immediately legible and
the solution directly serves it.
```
```
13 – 20 Addresses a real problem but the population or benefit is somewhat vague. The connection between solution
and impact exists but needs sharpening.
```
```
6 – 12 Problem is loosely defined or the solution doesn't clearly address it. Hard to tell who this is for or what changes
for them.
```
```
0 – 5 Problem is unclear or the solution has little connection to it.
```
##### TECHNICAL EXECUTION 30 PTS

```
25 – 30 Core functionality works and effectively demonstrates the idea. Uses AI in a way that feels purposeful — not
bolted on. Demo is clear and impressive.
```
```
16 – 24 Core functionality mostly works. AI is used adequately. Some gaps in the demo but the concept comes through.
```
```
6 – 15 Significant gaps in functionality or the demo doesn't clearly show how it works. AI use feels superficial or
incidental.
```
```
0 – 5 Doesn't work or is a mockup only. Little evidence of meaningful technical implementation.
```
##### ETHICAL ALIGNMENT 25 PTS

```
21 – 25 Team clearly wrestled with ethical implications. Can speak to who might be harmed, what could go wrong, and
what safeguards exist. Solution feels empowering.
```
```
13 – 20 Team considered some ethical implications and has reasonable answers. Solution is broadly empowering even
if not all risks are addressed.
```
```
6 – 12 Ethical considerations feel like an afterthought. Team struggles to articulate potential harms or hasn't built
meaningful safeguards.
```
```
0 – 5 No meaningful ethical consideration. Solution risks disempowering or harming users with no acknowledgement.
```
##### PRESENTATION 20 PTS

```
17 – 20 Clear, confident explanation of the problem, solution, and impact. Demo shows the product working. Q&A;
handled well.
```
```
11 – 16 Covers the key points. Demo is functional. Team communicates the core idea clearly even if presentation has
rough edges.
```
```
5 – 10 Hard to follow or key pieces are missing. Demo is unclear or incomplete. Struggles to answer questions.
```
```
0 – 4 Incoherent or incomplete. Can't demonstrate functionality or explain what they built.
```
